ANIMATED PAIN WORDS

BACKGROUND

This project began as a re-evaluation of current pain scales. Communicating pain accurately is a prevalent issue in the medical field. In an average doctor's visit scenario, there are two levels in which pain has to be communicated: First, the patient must describe their pain precisely. Second, the doctor must accurately interpret the pain the patient is in. Unfortunately, pain is subjective and very difficult to communicate or interpret exactly.

My project explored creating motion graphics for words that were commonly used to describe pain. I chose to add constraints to the project as it was very open-ended. The motion graphics would be purely typographical and only use black, white, and grey tones. The objective was to create accurate representations of the words so that most people could relate to them. Through the principle of isomorphic correspondence, where a feeling or emotion is evoked from a visual, I wanted my animated pain words to almost hurt the viewer.

INITIAL PROTOTYPES



USER TESTING & OBSERVATIONS: ROUND 1

Upon engaging in user testing, I noticed a major drawback to what I was asking people. For many people, when they saw the word on the screen, they already started to form a movement in their minds. Therefore, even if the animations that I'd created were fairly accurate enough, if the motions on screen didn't match the motions in the user's head, then they'd consider it to be inaccurate.

From here, I wondered if the animations would be successful if I replaced all the words with a simple shape, or a series of simple shapes. I selected the animation for "Cramping" to explore these ideas further and continue user testing on the way.

REFINED PROTOTYPES: EXPLORATIONS OF SHAPE

Unfortunately, simply replacing the word with a solid rectangle was not as successful as I'd hoped. Reflecting upon the original animation with the word, I realized that what made it successful was the negative shapes and forms within and between the letters.

REFINED PROTOTYPES: EXPLORATIONS OF MULTIPLES

REFINED PROTOTYPES: EXPLORATIONS OF LANGUAGE

Employing simple shapes and multiples of shapes proved to be unsuccessful. The resulting animations were not distinguishable of the word they were supposed to represent. I then explored applying the same motions to a translation of the word in another language. Therefore, the positive and negative spaces in the letterforms would be present for the movements to react with.


Unfortunately, when the user does not understand the word that's shown, the applied movements are not very obvious or defining.

In conclusion, the viewer needs to see the original word in order to be able to associate the motions with the correct feeling. With this in mind, I continued on my original path to animate all fifteen pain words.

FINAL ITERATIONS












CONCLUSION

In the end, I completed animated shorts for all fifteen of the pain words. Unfortunately, I ran out of time to user test the rest of them extensively, so they are not as polished as I'd like. In terms of next steps following this project, I'm working to continue user testing and refine my animations.

Through the completion of this project, I learned a lot about how people associate words with emotions, physical feelings, and visual motion.
