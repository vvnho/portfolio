STORIES OF SOLIDARITY

In May 2013, I was invited to participate in a Hack-a-thon with students and professors from both UC Davis and UCLA. The topic of the Hack-a-thon was how social media could be used to build new solidarity for those in insucure work. After an intense 24 hours of rapid prototyping and coding, we designed and created a social media platform/story sharing engine in which users can share any work related narrative. My role was to develop initial iterations of the website and mobile application and establish a core visual aesthetic.

Our application is unique for a few reasons. First, a user can post a story and remain anonymous if they wish. We understand that it is very important for our users to maintain a high level of privacy and we've done everything we can to ensure that. Second, our application pins stories on a map to show where the event or narrative is happening. In this way, a data analyst or researcher can observe areas with a greater amount of stories. Labor and community organizations can also use this data to target areas in greatest need of action.

The project was well received and the team moved from prototype to production. We met with labor organizations, data visualization researchers, developers, and design firms to bring our idea to realization. I worked to refine iterations of the web and mobile application and solidify branding in promotional pieces. In September 2013, our team was invited to the AFL-CIO convention to occupy a booth and tell conventioners about our project. To draw attention on the main floor of the convention center, I designed a short 1-minute introduction video to explain the application in brief.
